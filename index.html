I am a Ph.D. candidate in the Department of Chemistry at Princeton University, working with Prof. Joshua Rabinowitz. My research uses isotope tracing, mass-spectrometry-based metabolomics, and quantitative modeling to understand how cells allocate carbon and energy. I am especially interested in oleaginous yeasts and how nutrient availability rewires central metabolism toward lipid accumulation, with applications in sustainable bioproduction.

Publications

  1. Lipid accumulation in nitrogen and phosphorus-limited yeast is caused by less growth-related dilution

    Xi Li, Daniel R. Weilandt, Felix C. Keber, Arjuna M. Subramanian, Sebastian R. Loynes, Christopher V. Rao, et al.

    Metabolic Engineering, 2025.

    Abstract +

    Oleaginous yeasts accumulate large amounts of lipids when nutrients such as nitrogen or phosphorus are limited. Using isotope tracing and quantitative metabolic flux analysis in two oleaginous yeasts, we show that nitrogen or phosphorus limitation does not strongly upregulate lipid biosynthetic enzymes or fluxes. Instead, lipid accumulation is driven by reduced growth-related dilution: under nutrient limitation, biosynthetic capacity is preserved while biomass formation slows, allowing lipids to accumulate to high cellular fractions. The work clarifies the metabolic logic of microbial oleaginicity and informs strategies for engineering high-yield lipid production.

  2. Decompartmentalization of the yeast mitochondrial metabolism to improve chemical production in Issatchenkia orientalis

    Vinh G. Tran, Shih-I Tan, Huaiyu Xu, Daniel R. Weilandt, Xi Li, Saurabh S. Bhagwat, Zia Zhu, Jeremy S. Guest, Joshua D. Rabinowitz, Huimin Zhao.

    Nature Communications, 16, 7110, 2025.

    Abstract +

    Microbial chemical production is often limited by cofactor supply, a challenge magnified in yeasts whose cofactor metabolism is compartmentalized between cytosol and mitochondria. We rewire Issatchenkia orientalis by relocating mitochondrial NADH to the cytosol via cytosolic expression of pyruvate dehydrogenase and coupling a reductive TCA pathway with a glyoxylate shunt to conserve NADH. The engineered strain produces succinic acid at titers of 104 g/L and yields of 0.85 g/g glucose, surpassing the cytosolic NADH-limited theoretical maximum and demonstrating decompartmentalization as a powerful cofactor engineering strategy.

  3. Metabolic flux and resource balance in the oleaginous yeast Rhodotorula toruloides

    Eric J. Mooney, Patrick F. Suthers, Wheaton L. Schroeder, Hoang V. Dinh, Xi Li, Yihui Shen, Tianxia Xiao, et al.

    Metabolic Engineering, 94, 169–181, 2025.

    Abstract +

    Rhodotorula toruloides is a promising bioproduction host owing to high lipid yields and broad substrate range. We combine isotope-tracing metabolic flux analysis with proteomics to parameterize a genome-scale resource balance model (rtRBA). Strikingly, R. toruloides grows at rates comparable to S. cerevisiae while consuming about one-fifth as much glucose, routing carbon predominantly through the pentose phosphate pathway and TCA cycle. The integrated model rationalizes the organism's resource economy and provides a quantitative foundation for designing improved oleaginous strains.

  4. Engineering auxin degradation into root-associated bacteria promotes plant growth

    Ting Jiang, Yihui Shen, Xi Li, Michal J. Kozlowski, Philip D. Jeffrey, John T. Groves, Joshua D. Rabinowitz, Jonathan M. Conway.

    bioRxiv, 2025.

    Abstract +

    Overproduction of indole-3-acetic acid (IAA) by rhizosphere bacteria can disrupt auxin homeostasis and cause root growth inhibition. Variovorax bacteria mitigate this through the iad auxin-degradation locus. Using bacterial genetics, metabolomics, and isotope tracing, we assign roles to individual Iad enzymes and show that IadDE acts as a monooxygenase, not a Rieske dioxygenase, initiating catabolism via a novel intermediate. Installing chromosomal iad cassettes in root-associated commensals (Polaromonas and Paraburkholderia) yields the first engineered bacteria that reprogram rhizosphere auxin in complex environments, enhancing plant biomass without disrupting microbiome composition.

  5. Selected ion monitoring for orbitrap-based metabolomics

    Wenyun Lu, Matthew J. McBride, Won Dong Lee, Xi Xing, Xincheng Xu, Xi Li, Anna M. Oschmann, Yihui Shen, Caroline R. Bartman, Joshua D. Rabinowitz.

    Metabolites, 14(4), 184, 2024.

    Abstract +

    Orbitrap mass spectrometry in full-scan mode detects hundreds of metabolites simultaneously, but sensitivity remains limiting for low-abundance species and low-fractional-abundance isotope-labeled forms. We deploy selected ion monitoring (SIM), using the quadrupole as a mass filter, to enrich analytes of interest in the orbitrap. SIM substantially improves signal-to-noise, measurement precision, and isotope-ratio accuracy in tissue extracts. We also identify space-charge effects from over- accumulation of similar-m/z ions and provide guidelines to deploy SIM safely for quantitative metabolomics.

  6. Enzymatic biosynthesis and immobilization of polyprotein verified at the single-molecule level

    Yibing Deng, Tao Wu, Mengdi Wang, Sihan Shi, Guodong Yuan, Xi Li, Hai-Chao Chong, Bin Wu, Peng Zheng.

    Nature Communications, 10, 2775, 2019.

    Abstract +

    Polyproteins are widely used as substrates for single-molecule force spectroscopy but are challenging to construct with defined composition and order. We describe an enzymatic strategy that combines the strict ligase OaAEP1 with the TEV protease to build polyprotein chains with rationally controlled sequences and orientations, then immobilize them site-specifically on surfaces. AFM-based single-molecule force spectroscopy verifies the composition of the resulting polymers, providing a versatile platform for mechanical studies of designed protein assemblies.

  7. In situ shape control of thermoplasmonic gold nanostars on oxide substrates for hyperthermia-mediated cell detachment

    Gail A. Vinnacombe-Willson, Naihao Chiang, Leonardo Scarabelli, Yao Hu, Liv K. Heidenreich, Xi Li, et al.

    ACS Central Science, 6(11), 2105–2116, 2020.

    Abstract +

    We report a microfluidic approach for the direct, in situ growth of anisotropic gold nanostars on indium tin oxide and glass substrates. The synthesis yields highly branched, uniform, and dense nanostar arrays with strong light-to-heat conversion under laser irradiation. The resulting thermoplasmonic substrates enable spatially controlled hyperthermia and demonstrate non-invasive, light-triggered detachment of mammalian cells, with implications for cell harvesting, patterning, and substrate-based photothermal applications.

  8. Single-molecule force spectroscopy reveals that iron–ligand bonds modulate proteins in different modes

    Guodong Yuan, Huaxing Liu, Qun Ma, Xi Li, Jingyuan Nie, Jinglin Zuo, Peng Zheng.

    The Journal of Physical Chemistry Letters, 10, 5428–5433, 2019.

    Abstract +

    Iron coordinates to proteins through several distinct interactions: Fe–O (Glu/Asp), Fe–N (His), and Fe–S (Cys). We use AFM-based single-molecule force spectroscopy to dissect these bonds in superoxide reductase, which contains an Fe(His)4Cys–Glu center. The three bond types rupture under markedly different forces and modulate protein mechanical stability in distinct modes, providing a bond-resolved view of how metal–ligand chemistry tunes metalloprotein mechanics.

  9. Multistep protein unfolding scenarios from the rupture of a complex metal cluster Cd3S9

    Guodong Yuan, Qun Ma, Tao Wu, Mengdi Wang, Xi Li, Jinglin Zuo, Peng Zheng.

    Scientific Reports, 9, 10518, 2019.

    Abstract +

    We use AFM-based single-molecule force spectroscopy to probe unfolding of the β-domain of human metallothionein-3, which contains a Cd3S9 cluster. We resolve four distinct three-step unfolding scenarios attributable to different orders of Cd–S bond rupture within the cluster, illustrating how complex metal–ligand networks give rise to multistep mechanical unfolding pathways in metalloproteins.
